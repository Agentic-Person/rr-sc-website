## 3. Roofing Materials Guide

### Asphalt Shingles

Asphalt shingles are the most popular roofing material in the United States and the most common choice for Charleston-area homes. They are available in three main categories.

**Three-Tab Shingles** are the most basic and affordable option. They feature a flat, uniform appearance with a single layer of material. Three-tab shingles typically last 15 to 20 years and carry wind ratings of 60 to 70 mph. While they are the most budget-friendly option, they are generally not recommended for coastal South Carolina homes due to their lower wind resistance.

**Architectural Shingles** (also called dimensional or laminate shingles) are the most popular choice for Lowcountry homes. They feature a multi-layered construction that creates a dimensional, textured appearance. Architectural shingles are thicker and heavier than three-tab shingles, providing better wind resistance (110 to 130 mph for standard products, up to 150 mph for premium lines) and a longer lifespan of 25 to 30 years. They are available in a wide range of colors and styles.

**Luxury or Designer Shingles** are the premium tier of asphalt shingles, designed to replicate the look of natural slate or cedar shake at a fraction of the cost and weight. They offer the highest wind ratings (up to 130-150 mph), the longest warranties (up to lifetime/50 years), and the most dramatic aesthetic impact. Luxury shingles are an excellent choice for homeowners who want a high-end look without the expense and structural requirements of natural slate or tile.

**Pros of Asphalt Shingles:** Most affordable roofing option, wide variety of colors and styles, easy to install and repair, readily available, compatible with most roof pitches and styles.

**Cons of Asphalt Shingles:** Shorter lifespan than metal, slate, or tile. Susceptible to algae growth in humid climates (look for algae-resistant shingles). Lower wind resistance than metal roofing. Granule loss accelerates in salt air environments.

**Cost Range:** $3.50 to $7.00 per square foot installed, depending on the product grade and roof complexity.

**Best Use in Coastal SC:** Architectural shingles with a minimum 130 mph wind rating and algae-resistant granules are the recommended asphalt option for Lowcountry homes.

### Metal Roofing

Metal roofing has become increasingly popular in the Charleston area due to its superior performance in coastal conditions. Several metal roofing systems are available.

**Standing Seam Metal Roofing** features long vertical panels connected by raised seams. The seams are elevated above the roof surface, which means fasteners are concealed and water runs off without encountering any penetrations. Standing seam is the premium metal roofing option and offers the best wind and water resistance. It is rated for winds of 140 mph or higher and can last 40 to 70 years.

**Metal Shingle Systems** are designed to look like traditional shingles, slate, or tile while providing the durability benefits of metal. They are installed in interlocking panels and offer wind ratings comparable to standing seam systems.

**Corrugated Metal Roofing** features wavy or ribbed panels and is a more affordable metal option. It is commonly used on porches, additions, and secondary structures. Corrugated metal is durable and long-lasting but has exposed fasteners that may require periodic maintenance.

**Pros of Metal Roofing:** Exceptional wind resistance (140 mph+), longest lifespan of common roofing materials (40-70 years), reflects solar heat and reduces cooling costs by 10-25%, lightweight (reduces structural load), fire-resistant (Class A rating), environmentally friendly (often made from recycled materials, 100% recyclable at end of life), resists mold, mildew, and insect damage.

**Cons of Metal Roofing:** Higher upfront cost than asphalt shingles. Can dent from large hail or falling branches. Expansion and contraction with temperature changes requires proper installation techniques. Rain noise (mitigated by proper underlayment and insulation). Limited availability of qualified installers.

**Cost Range:** $7.00 to $14.00 per square foot installed for standing seam, $6.00 to $10.00 for metal shingles, $4.00 to $8.00 for corrugated metal.

**Best Use in Coastal SC:** Standing seam metal roofing with Galvalume or aluminum construction and a Kynar 500 (PVDF) paint finish is the gold standard for coastal performance. The concealed fastener system eliminates leak points, and the smooth surface sheds debris and resists algae growth. Aluminum standing seam is preferred over steel for homes within a few miles of the ocean due to superior salt air corrosion resistance.

### Slate Roofing

Natural slate is a premium roofing material quarried from stone. It is found on many historic homes in Charleston's downtown neighborhoods and is prized for its beauty, durability, and longevity.

**Pros of Slate Roofing:** Extraordinary lifespan (75-200 years depending on slate grade), unmatched natural beauty, fire-resistant, environmentally friendly (natural material, extremely long life), excellent wind resistance when properly installed, impervious to rot, mold, and insects.

**Cons of Slate Roofing:** Very high cost, extremely heavy (requires reinforced roof structure), fragile when walked on (complicates maintenance and repairs), limited number of qualified installers, long lead times for sourcing material.

**Cost Range:** $15.00 to $35.00 per square foot installed.

**Best Use in Coastal SC:** Slate is best suited for historic home restoration, luxury new construction, and homes where the owner prioritizes longevity and aesthetic appeal above all else. The weight of slate requires a roof structure specifically engineered to support it. In the salt air environment of coastal South Carolina, slate itself is unaffected by corrosion, but the metal flashing and fasteners used with slate must be carefully chosen for corrosion resistance (copper or stainless steel is recommended).

### Clay and Concrete Tile Roofing

Tile roofing, in both clay and concrete varieties, offers a distinctive Mediterranean or Old World aesthetic along with excellent durability.

**Clay Tiles** are kiln-fired from natural clay and are available in barrel (mission), flat, and interlocking profiles. They are extremely durable, fade-resistant, and can last 75 to 100 years.

**Concrete Tiles** are molded from a mixture of cement, sand, and water and can replicate the look of clay tile, slate, or wood shake at a lower cost. Concrete tiles are heavier than clay and typically last 40 to 50 years.

**Pros of Tile Roofing:** Excellent durability and longevity, fire-resistant (Class A), withstands high winds when properly installed, wide range of styles and colors, good thermal performance.

**Cons of Tile Roofing:** Heavy (requires reinforced roof structure), higher cost than asphalt, individual tiles can crack from foot traffic or impact, concrete tiles may fade over time in the Lowcountry sun.

**Cost Range:** $10.00 to $20.00 per square foot installed for clay, $8.00 to $15.00 for concrete.

**Best Use in Coastal SC:** Tile roofing is well-suited for Mediterranean, Spanish Colonial, and other traditional architectural styles found in some Charleston-area neighborhoods. Like slate, tile is impervious to salt air and biological growth, but the underlayment beneath the tile must be high quality because water can be driven under tiles during heavy rain with high wind.

### Flat Roofing Systems (TPO, EPDM, Modified Bitumen)

Flat or low-slope roofing requires specialized materials that form a continuous waterproof membrane, since shingles and tiles rely on gravity and overlap to shed water and cannot perform on low-slope surfaces.

**TPO (Thermoplastic Polyolefin)** is a single-ply roofing membrane that is heat-welded at the seams for a watertight bond. It is white or light-colored, reflecting solar heat and reducing cooling costs. TPO is the most popular flat roofing material for residential applications and typically lasts 15 to 25 years.

**EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer)** is a synthetic rubber membrane that is extremely durable and flexible. It is typically black, which can increase cooling costs, though white EPDM is available. EPDM lasts 20 to 30 years and is known for its resistance to UV radiation and temperature extremes.

**Modified Bitumen** is an asphalt-based system applied in multiple layers for a robust, waterproof surface. It is more forgiving of minor installation imperfections than single-ply systems and is a good choice for flat roofs that receive foot traffic. Modified bitumen typically lasts 15 to 20 years.

**Pros of Flat Roofing:** More affordable than sloped roof materials per square foot, relatively quick installation, easy to access for maintenance, good choice for additions, porches, and modern architectural designs.

**Cons of Flat Roofing:** Shorter lifespan than sloped roofing materials, requires diligent drainage maintenance to prevent ponding water, more susceptible to puncture and damage from foot traffic, seams can fail over time.

**Cost Range:** $5.00 to $10.00 per square foot installed for TPO, $4.00 to $8.00 for EPDM, $4.00 to $9.00 for modified bitumen.

**Best Use in Coastal SC:** TPO is the recommended flat roofing material for most Lowcountry homes due to its reflective properties (reducing cooling costs in the hot, humid climate), strong seam integrity from heat welding, and resistance to mold and algae growth.

### Cedar Shake and Shingle Roofing

Cedar shake and shingle roofing provides a natural, rustic aesthetic that complements Lowcountry architectural styles. Cedar shakes are hand-split for a rough, textured appearance, while cedar shingles are machine-sawn for a smoother, more uniform look.

**Pros of Cedar Roofing:** Beautiful natural appearance that weathers to a silver-gray patina, good insulating properties, natural resistance to insects (when untreated), lightweight compared to slate and tile, wind-resistant when properly installed.

**Cons of Cedar Roofing:** Requires regular maintenance (cleaning, treatment) to prevent moss, mold, and rot, fire risk unless treated with fire-retardant, shorter lifespan in the humid Lowcountry climate (20-30 years versus 30-50 years in drier climates), higher maintenance cost than asphalt shingles, may be restricted or prohibited by some HOAs and fire codes.

**Cost Range:** $8.00 to $14.00 per square foot installed.

**Best Use in Coastal SC:** Cedar roofing can be beautiful on Lowcountry homes, but the Charleston area's high humidity, heavy rainfall, and biological growth (moss, algae, mildew) make cedar a high-maintenance choice. Homeowners who choose cedar should be committed to regular cleaning and treatment (every 2-3 years) and should select pressure-treated or fire-retardant-treated cedar. Cedar is not recommended for homes in the most exposed coastal locations where salt spray is constant.

## 5. Insurance Claims Process

### How Roof Insurance Claims Work

When a roof is damaged by a storm, hail, fallen tree, or other covered event, the homeowner's insurance policy typically covers the cost of repair or replacement, minus the policy deductible. The insurance claims process can be complex and time-consuming, which is why Restoration Roofing SC provides hands-on assistance to guide homeowners through every step.

### Step-by-Step Insurance Claims Process

**Step 1: Document the Damage.** After a storm or damage event, document the damage with photographs and notes. Do not attempt to climb on the roof. Take photos from the ground and from inside the home (if there is interior water damage). Save any pieces of roofing material that have blown off.

**Step 2: Contact Restoration Roofing SC.** Call Restoration Roofing SC at (843) 306-2939 as soon as possible after discovering damage. The team will schedule a free inspection and damage assessment, typically within 24 to 48 hours (sooner in emergency situations).

**Step 3: Professional Inspection and Documentation.** A Restoration Roofing SC inspector will perform a thorough roof inspection, documenting all damage with detailed photographs, measurements, and notes. This professional documentation is critical for a successful insurance claim because it ensures no damage is overlooked and provides the evidence the insurance company needs to process the claim.

**Step 4: File the Insurance Claim.** The homeowner contacts their insurance company to file a claim. Restoration Roofing SC can help the homeowner understand what to say and what information to provide. It is important to file the claim promptly, as most insurance policies have time limits for reporting damage.

**Step 5: Insurance Adjuster Inspection.** The insurance company will send an adjuster to inspect the damage. Restoration Roofing SC strongly recommends having a company representative present during the adjuster's visit. This ensures that all damage is identified and that the adjuster's assessment is complete and accurate. The Restoration Roofing SC representative can point out damage that the adjuster might miss and can answer technical questions about the roofing system.

**Step 6: Review the Insurance Company's Estimate.** After the adjuster's inspection, the insurance company will issue an estimate for the cost of repairs. Restoration Roofing SC will review this estimate and compare it to their own assessment. If the insurance estimate is lower than the actual cost of repairs, the team will work with the insurance company to supplement the claim with additional documentation.

**Step 7: Approve and Schedule Repairs.** Once the claim is settled, the homeowner approves the scope of work and Restoration Roofing SC schedules the repair or replacement. The homeowner is responsible only for their insurance deductible. Restoration Roofing SC works directly with the insurance company for payment of the covered amount.

### Salt Air Corrosion and Coastal Roofing

Homes near the coast in Charleston, Mount Pleasant, Sullivans Island, Isle of Palms, Folly Beach, and other barrier island and waterfront communities face accelerated corrosion from salt-laden air. Salt air affects roofing materials in several ways. Metal roofing components (flashing, fasteners, drip edge) corrode faster when exposed to salt air. Galvanized steel, which is standard in many roofing products, may develop rust within 5 to 10 years in heavy salt air environments. Metal roofs should use aluminum, stainless steel, or copper components in coastal locations. Asphalt shingle roofs may experience accelerated granule loss in salt air, reducing the shingle's lifespan and UV protection. Galvalume (aluminum-zinc alloy coated steel) standing seam roofing is more corrosion-resistant than galvanized steel but may still corrode in the most extreme coastal environments. Pure aluminum or copper is the most corrosion-resistant option for coastal metal roofing.

Restoration Roofing SC specifies coastal-grade materials and fasteners for homes in salt air environments, ensuring that the entire roofing system, not just the visible surface material, is engineered to withstand the Lowcountry's corrosive coastal atmosphere.

### Humidity and Moisture Effects on Roofing

Charleston's humid subtropical climate, with average relative humidity often exceeding 70-80%, creates unique challenges for roofing systems. High humidity promotes the growth of algae, moss, and mildew on roof surfaces, particularly on the north-facing slopes that receive less direct sunlight. Black streaks on asphalt shingle roofs are typically caused by Gloeocapsa magma, a blue-green algae that feeds on the limestone filler in shingle granules. Algae-resistant shingles, which contain copper or zinc granules, are strongly recommended for all Lowcountry homes.

Humidity also affects the attic space beneath the roof. Without proper ventilation, moisture can accumulate in the attic, leading to condensation on the underside of the roof deck, mold and mildew growth on sheathing and framing, deterioration of insulation, and premature aging of roofing materials from underneath. Restoration Roofing SC ensures every roofing installation includes proper ridge ventilation, soffit ventilation, or a combination of intake and exhaust ventilation to maintain healthy airflow through the attic space.

### Wind Resistance Ratings

Wind resistance is one of the most important performance characteristics for a roof in the Charleston area. Roofing materials are tested and rated for wind resistance according to several standards. ASTM D3161 (Standard Test Method for Wind-Resistance of Steep Slope Roofing Products) classifies shingles as Class A (60 mph), Class D (90 mph), or Class F (110 mph). ASTM D7158 (Standard Test Method for Wind Resistance of Sealed Asphalt Shingles) classifies shingles as Class D (90 mph), Class G (120 mph), or Class H (150 mph). UL 580 rates roofing assemblies for uplift resistance (Class 30, 60, or 90) based on negative pressure testing.

For the Charleston area, Restoration Roofing SC recommends a minimum wind rating of 130 mph for all new roof installations. The South Carolina Building Code requires enhanced wind resistance for homes in the coastal zone, including specific nail patterns, underlayment requirements, and material ratings. Restoration Roofing SC ensures all installations meet or exceed these requirements.

## 13. Why Coastal Roofing Requires Local Expertise

## 14. Roof Ventilation

### Why Ventilation Matters

Proper roof ventilation is critical for the longevity of a roofing system, especially in the hot, humid Charleston climate. A well-ventilated attic allows hot air to escape through exhaust vents at or near the ridge, draws in cooler outside air through intake vents at or near the soffits, reduces moisture buildup that can cause mold, rot, and insulation damage, lowers attic temperatures (which can exceed 150 degrees in a Lowcountry summer), extends the life of roofing materials by reducing thermal stress, and reduces cooling costs by preventing excessive heat transfer from the attic to the living space.

### Types of Ventilation

**Ridge Vents** are installed along the peak of the roof and provide continuous exhaust ventilation. They are the most effective and aesthetically clean exhaust ventilation method.

**Soffit Vents** are installed in the soffit (the underside of the roof overhang) and provide intake ventilation. They work in conjunction with ridge vents to create natural airflow through the attic.

**Powered Attic Ventilators** use an electric fan to actively exhaust hot air from the attic. They can be effective but add an ongoing electricity cost and may create negative pressure issues if not properly sized.

**Gable Vents** are installed in the gable end walls of the attic and can serve as both intake and exhaust ventilation. They are less effective than a ridge-and-soffit system but can supplement existing ventilation.

Restoration Roofing SC evaluates the ventilation system during every roof inspection and ensures that all new installations include adequate balanced ventilation for the Lowcountry climate.

## 15. Signs You Need Roof Attention

### Interior Warning Signs

Water stains on ceilings or walls, peeling paint near the roofline, mold or mildew in the attic, daylight visible through the roof boards, sagging ceiling areas, higher than normal energy bills, and musty odors in upper floors or the attic are all signs that the roof may need professional attention.

### Exterior Warning Signs

Missing, cracked, curling, or buckling shingles, excessive granule loss (visible in gutters or at the base of downspouts), damaged or missing flashing around chimneys, vents, and skylights, sagging or uneven areas on the roof surface, moss, algae, or fungal growth, damaged or missing ridge caps, rusted or deteriorated metal components, and clogged, damaged, or overflowing gutters are all signs that a professional roof inspection is warranted.

### Age-Related Indicators

If an asphalt shingle roof is more than 20 years old, it is approaching the end of its expected service life and should be inspected annually. Even if no obvious problems are visible, an aging roof may have hidden vulnerabilities that a professional inspection can identify. Proactive replacement before a failure occurs is always less expensive and less disruptive than emergency replacement after a leak or storm damage.

## 16. Energy Efficiency and Roofing

### Cool Roofing in the Lowcountry

In the Charleston area, where air conditioning costs are a major component of home energy expenses, the choice of roofing material and color can significantly impact energy efficiency. Cool roofing refers to roofing materials that reflect more sunlight and absorb less heat than standard materials.

**Metal Roofing** is inherently reflective, especially in lighter colors, and can reduce cooling costs by 10-25% compared to dark asphalt shingles. Metal roofing with an Energy Star-rated reflective coating provides the best energy performance.

**Light-Colored Asphalt Shingles** reflect more solar energy than dark-colored shingles. Some manufacturers offer Cool Roof-rated asphalt shingles with specially designed granules that reflect infrared radiation.

**TPO and White EPDM** flat roofing materials are highly reflective and are excellent choices for flat or low-slope roof sections in terms of energy efficiency.

Restoration Roofing SC can advise on the most energy-efficient roofing options for your home and help you calculate the potential energy savings of different materials and colors.
